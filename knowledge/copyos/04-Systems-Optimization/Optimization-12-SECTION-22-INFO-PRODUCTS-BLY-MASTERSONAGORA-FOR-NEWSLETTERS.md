## **SECTION 2.2 — INFO PRODUCTS (BLY + MASTERSON/AGORA — FOR NEWSLETTERS, COURSES, AND MEMBERSHIPS)**

---

**Info Products are a specific category** (they are knowledge-based — courses, newsletters, ebooks, memberships, and coaching programs).

**The differences from physical products/services:**

- **The margin:** 90-98% (they are digital — a one-time production cost of R$500-5k, with infinite sales at zero marginal cost, vs. the 30-50% margins of physical products with their COGS)
- **The delivery:** Instant (a download, an email for access — an immediate gratification vs. the 3-7 day shipping for physical products)
- **The scalability:** Infinite (1 customer or 100,000 is the same infrastructure, vs. the complex scaling of a physical product's inventory/fulfillment)
- **The refund:** A digital refund is easy (the perception — "they've already consumed the content and are asking for a refund?" is an ethical gray area, vs. the hassle of return shipping for a physical product which filters refunds)

---

### **THE COPY CHARACTERISTICS OF INFO PRODUCTS (From Masterson/Agora's Decades of Practice):**

---

**CHARACTERISTIC #1 — IT'S EDUCATION-HEAVY (90% Value, 10% Pitch — from Abraham):**

**Agora Financial** (newsletters from $49-5,000/year):

Their daily emails (365/year) are 90% educational (market analysis, investment ideas, economic trends — standalone value) + 10% promotional (for special reports, premium newsletters, events — only a pitch).

**Why 90/10 works:**

**For extreme reciprocity** (from Doc 1 Section 2.1 — you've given 9x the value before you ask for 1x, a massive obligation, so WHEN the pitch comes it's accepted because of the trust earned from the previous 9 emails).

**For authority building** (teaching daily positions you as an expert, not a vendor — Kennedy's trust equation, high credibility).

**For list growth** (consistent value leads to organic referrals, a low 0.8%/month unsub rate vs. the industry's 2-5%, and a net positive growth of 5-10%/month — which is sustainable for decades).

---

**The copy's implication:**

**The sales pages for info products:** Are long-form (6,000-12,000 words — the Perfect Promo structure from Doc 5).

**Why are they so long:**

- **For an educational preview** (the prospect wants a SAMPLE of the content's quality — a long copy is a free seminar, it demonstrates expertise, and delivers tangible value before the purchase)
- **For justification** (info is intangible, there's skepticism like "is it worth paying for knowledge I can Google for free?" — a long copy PROVES the knowledge is unique/organized/actionable, and that Google is not systematic)
- **For reciprocity** (6-12k words of free education is the equivalent of R$2-5k in consulting value — Abraham's 10:1, you've given 10x before you ask, a strong obligation to purchase)

**Schwartz:** "Long is GOOD for info products (the education IS a preview of the product — the more you give for free, the more they will want the complete paid version)."

---

**CHARACTERISTIC #2 — LAUNCH SEQUENCES (Jeff Walker's PLF — The Sideways Sales Letter):**

**The typical structure of an info product launch:**

```
WEEK 1 — THE PRE-PRE-LAUNCH (To Seed the Opportunity):
• Email 1 (Day 1): "I discovered something about the [topic] market..." (an opportunity reveal, not the product)
• Email 2 (Day 3): Video #1 "Why 89% of [this group] are stuck with [this problem]" (a diagnosis, pain awareness)
• Email 3 (Day 5): A roundup of the comments (community engagement, building social proof)

The value delivered: A free diagnosis (a self-check, to identify their gaps — it's actionable)

WEEK 2 — THE PRE-LAUNCH (Deep Education):
• Email 4 (Day 8): Video #2 "The solution's approach [the mechanism]" (to teach the framework, a high-level of HOW it works)
• Email 5 (Day 10): A case study of [a client] (a transformation using the mechanism, proof of the pattern)
• Email 6 (Day 12): Video #3 "The implementation roadmap" (a week-by-week breakdown, tangible and realistic)
• Email 7 (Day 14): An FAQ preview (the objections are addressed before the pitch, for preemption)

The value delivered: The framework is taught (it can be applied for free with DIY), the case study is inspiring (it's replicable), the roadmap is clear (the expectations are realistic) — a R$2-3k consulting equivalent for FREE.

WEEK 3 — THE LAUNCH (The Cart is Open for 72h):
• Email 8 (Day 15 — 09:00): The CART OPEN announcement (the complete offer stack is presented, the pricing, the guarantee, the urgency of a 72h deadline)
• Email 9 (Day 15 — 18:00): A video of testimonials (3-5 clients on-camera with their transformations, for authenticity)
• Email 10 (Day 16 — 09:00): An additional case study (from a different niche, for the universality of the pattern)
• Email 11 (Day 16 — 18:00): A comprehensive FAQ (the top 10 objections are answered in detail)
• Email 12 (Day 17 — 09:00): Urgency (24h remaining, a countdown, a spot update of 12 remaining, a quantified loss for delaying)
• Email 13 (Day 17 — 18:00): THE FINAL CALL (6h remaining, extreme FOMO, a literal last chance)

WEEK 3 — THE POST-LAUNCH (The Cart is Closed):
• Email 14 (Day 18): A sold-out confirmation (100/100 spots are filled, the scarcity was REAL as it was enforced, the next cohort is on [date] with a wait list)

THE TOTAL SEQUENCE: 14 emails, 3 videos (30-45 min of total content), 17 days from start to finish.

THE TYPICAL CONVERSION: 20-40% (of the email list subscribers → purchasers — the HIGHEST method for info products because of the extreme reciprocity of 2 weeks of value + the built-up anticipation + the real scarcity + an informed decision after 14 days of education).

[A launch sequence is Walker's "Sideways Sales Letter" — instead of a single vertical 10k-word page, it's a horizontal sequence of 14 emails over time, with an intentional psychological buildup, the final event is the climax, and the conversion is 5-10x that of a single promotional email]
```

**Jeff Walker** (Product Launch Formula):

"Launch sequences work because of:

**Reciprocity** (you've given massive value for 2 weeks for free — an obligation),

**Anticipation** (a 14-day buildup — the excitement is a crescendo, they can't wait for the cart to open),

**Real scarcity** (the cart closes on the stated date, and it's enforced — it's not a perpetual fake evergreen),

**An informed decision** (they've been educated for 14 days, the buyers KNOW the product, it's not an impulse purchase they'll regret — the refunds are lower, the satisfaction is higher, and the LTV increases).

A 20-40% conversion vs. a single email's 1-3% is a **10x difference** for a sequence vs. a single email (the investment of 14 days, writing 14 emails, and producing 3 videos is worth the dramatic ROI)."

**CHARACTERISTIC #3 — THE PRICING SWEET SPOTS ($97-997 is Typical):**

**Info product pricing tiers:**

```
THE LOW-END ($7-47 — A Tripwire/Impulse):
• Ebooks (50-150 pages)
• Mini-courses (3-5 video modules)
• Checklists/Template packs
• The goal: To convert a prospect → a buyer (Cialdini's micro-commitment), and to partially recover the CAC
• The conversion: 8-18% (a low-ticket impulse buy, a minimal barrier)

THE MID-TIER ($97-497 — Core Info Products):
• Complete courses (10-20 modules, 20-40h of content)
• A monthly membership ($97-297/month — for continuity revenue)
• Recorded workshops (2-4h of intensive training)
• The goal: The main revenue, with profitable margins and a moderate LTV
• The conversion: 5-15% (a considered purchase, the ROI is calculated, the reviews are checked)

THE HIGH-END ($997-2,997 — Premium):
• Implementation programs (a course + group coaching calls)
• Masterminds (for 12 months, with an exclusive community and mentoring)
• Certification programs (a credential upon completion, for career value)
• The goal: To maximize the LTV, for a premium positioning, and a deep transformation
• The conversion: 3-10% (a significant investment, a careful decision, extensive proof is needed)

THE ULTRA-HIGH ($5,000-50,000 — Elite):
• 1-on-1 coaching/mentoring (personalized, high-touch)
• Done-with-you (an implementation together, with hand-holding)
• Elite masterminds (a maximum of 25-50 people, for ultra-high-net-worth networking)
• The goal: The apex of the value ladder, with whale clients (1 customer = the revenue of 10-100 regular ones)
• The conversion: 0.5-3% (an application funnel, strict qualification, a human-closed call)
```

**Masterson** (from Agora's practice):

"The optimal pricing for info products is in the $97-997 range (the impulse ceiling is $47, considered purchases are $97-497, and premium is $997-2,997 — these are the sweet spots from decades of testing, the conversion × the volume × the margin is optimal in these tiers; below $97 is high volume BUT a thin margin, above $2,997 is a high margin BUT low volume, and $97-997 is the balanced Goldilocks zone)."

**CHARACTERISTIC #4 — THE REFUND RATES (The Digital Paradox):**

**Physical products:** A 2-8% refund rate (the hassle of shipping, they keep it even if it's not perfect — friction with returns).

**Digital products:** A 5-15% refund rate (an easy refund — an email saying "I want a refund," a click on a link, it's instant — zero friction, some abuse it, but the majority are legitimately dissatisfied).

---

**To mitigate info product refunds:**

**Tactic #1 — A Conditional Guarantee (To Filter the Non-Serious):**

"A 90-day guarantee: IF you complete modules 1-12 (100% tracked), attend 4+ of the 6 calls, and submit the assignments for each — AND you don't get [the result], you'll get a 100% refund + you can keep the bonuses.

It's conditional to filter them (tire-kickers will read 'work required' and self-select out — only the committed will enter, and the committed will apply it, and applying it usually gets results, which lowers the refunds)."

**Agora's data:** A conditional guarantee has a 3-6% refund rate (vs. 8-12% for an unconditional one — the filtering works, it protects the margins).

---

**Tactic #2 — A Strong Onboarding (For Immediate Engagement):**

**The first 7 days are critical** (the Indoctrination from Doc 10 Section 3.3):

-   Email #1 is instant: It delivers the content (an immediate satisfaction, a perception of "I got value")
-   A quick win on day 3: An actionable technique (a small, fast result — for momentum, an increase in commitment)
-   A community invite on day 5: For belonging (Cialdini's Unity — they are less likely to refund if they are a member of the community, for the social ties)

**The completion rate:** 70-85% with a strong indoctrination (vs. 40-60% with a weak one) — the completion correlates with retention (completed = engaged = satisfied = no refund).

---

**Tactic #3 — Segment the Refunds (To Learn the Patterns):**

**An analysis of 100 refunds:**

-   **Reason #1 (40%):** "I didn't have time to complete it" (they didn't apply it, their life was busy — it's legitimate, not a product issue)
-   **Reason #2 (25%):** "It was too advanced for me" (a mismatch of the level — a targeting issue, not a product quality one)
-   **Reason #3 (20%):** "I didn't get the results" (they applied it BUT the results were insufficient — a product issue OR a mismatch of expectations)
-   **Reason #4 (10%):** "Technical issues" (the videos didn't play, the downloads failed — a fixable support issue)
-   **Reason #5 (5%):** Abuse (they consumed 100% and then refunded — the policy can be tightened, OR it can be accepted as a cost of doing business)

**The patterns for action:**

-   For reasons #1-2: Improve the targeting (qualify them better, set realistic expectations pre-purchase)
-   For reason #3: Improve the product (optimize the content quality and the results delivery)
-   For reason #4: Improve the support (proactively resolve tech issues)
-   For reason #5: Accept it (5% abuse is a cost of business, fighting it is bad PR, let it go)

**A refund rate of <8%** is healthy for info products (Agora/Empiricus's benchmark — from decades of data, 8% is acceptable, below that is excellent, and above that is a problem with the product/targeting).
