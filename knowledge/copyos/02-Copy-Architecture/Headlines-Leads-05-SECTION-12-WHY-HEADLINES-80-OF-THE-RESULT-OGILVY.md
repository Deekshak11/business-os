## **SECTION 1.2 — WHY HEADLINES = 80% OF THE RESULT (OGILVY)**

---

**David Ogilvy** (Confessions of an Advertising Man, chapter on headlines):

> *"On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy.*
>
> *When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar.*
>
> *If the headline fails, the body copy is wasted (it will never be read)."*

---

### **OGILVY'S RESEARCH (Empirical Validation):**

**Test 1 (Magazine Ads, 1960s):**

**Ad A:**
- Headline: Strong (benefit clear, specific, curiosity)
- Body: Identical in both ads
- Readership: **22% read the body** (after being hooked by the headline)

**Ad B:**
- Headline: Weak (vague, boring, generic)
- Body: Identical
- Readership: **4% read the body**

**Difference:** 22% vs. 4% = **5.5x** (from the headline alone — the body never changed).

**Implication:** A weak headline = 82% of potential readers are LOST (18% vs. 100% — the effort on the body copy is wasted).

---

**Test 2 (Newspaper Classifieds, Schwab data):**

1,000 classifieds analyzed (want ads, real estate, etc.).

**Top 10% of headlines** (benefit clear, specific): Average response of 3.2%.

**Bottom 10% of headlines** (vague, boring): Average response of 0.4%.

**An 8x difference** (from headline quality alone).

---

**Eye-Tracking Studies** (Modern — Nielsen Norman Group, 2000s-2020s):

Web pages: Users scan (they don't read linearly — F-pattern).

**Fixation data:**
- Headline: 80-95% fixate (they always look at it)
- Subhead: 60-75% (if the headline is compelling)
- Body paragraphs: 20-40% (declining — fewer people read each paragraph)
- Bullets: 50-70% (visual, scannable — higher than prose)

**Conclusion:** The headline is the gatekeeper (it lets people in or blocks them — the accessibility of the body depends on the headline's success).

---

### **INVESTMENT PROPORTION (Time/Effort):**

**A common error among copywriters:**

10% of time on the headline (1-2 hours, "good enough").

90% of time on the body (8-10 hours, extensive polishing).

**Result:** A brilliant body (that is never read — a weak headline blocked 80% of readers).

The waste: 90% of the effort (8-10h on the body) is applied to 20% of the audience (only those who got past the weak headline).

---

**The correct proportion (Ogilvy/Hopkins/Halbert consensus):**

**40-60% of time on the headline:**
- Generate 20-30 variations (2-3 hours)
- Score each one (30 min)
- Refine the top 10 (1-2 hours)
- Test the top 3-5 (2-4 days if A/B testing)
- Choose the winner (based on data)

**Total:** 6-10 hours on the headline (seems like a lot, BUT it's justified).

---

**40-60% of time on the body:**
- Structure (Doc 5 blueprint — 1-2h)
- Write sections (3-5h)
- Compile proof (1-2h)
- Polish (1h)

**Total:** 6-10 hours on the body.

---

**The balance:** Equal effort on the headline and body (not the common 10/90 imbalance).

---

**Ogilvy (extreme emphasis):**

> *"I spend WEEKS on headlines sometimes.*
>
> *Not days. WEEKS.*
>
> *I can write the body copy in days (the structure is known, the headline did the heavy lifting of persuasion).*
>
> *But the headline? It's critical.*
>
> *I will spend whatever time is necessary (weeks if needed) until the headline WORKS.*
>
> *Eighty cents of the dollar = 80% of the effort is justified (at a minimum)."*

---

**Halbert (practical validation):**

"The headline is an ad for the ad (the headline sells the body copy, the body copy sells the product).

A weak headline = nobody reads the body (the body is irrelevant, wasted).

A strong headline = many read the body (the body has an opportunity to convert).

**I spend 50-70% of my time on headlines** (no exaggeration — it's justified, the ROI has been proven for decades)."

---

### **PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:**

**Implication #1 — Generate Multiples (Don't Settle for the First One):**

**Don't:** Write 1-2 headlines, choose the "best" one, and use it.

**Do:** Write 20-30, score them all, test the top 5; the winner is the best of 30 (not the best of 2).

**Exploration matters** (the first ideas are rarely the best — the creative process needs quantity to liberate quality).

---

**Implication #2 — Always Test (When Possible):**

**Don't:** Guess which headline is better (intuition has an accuracy of ~60% — slightly better than a coin flip).

**Do:** Test (a small A/B test, a $50-200 budget, measure CTR — data has an accuracy of 85-95%).

**Hopkins:** "Tested advertising beats guessed advertising 10:1 (in revenue difference — a test costs $100 and prevents a $10k waste on the wrong headline)."

---

**Implication #3 — Write Headlines After the Body is Written:**

The sequence: Write the body → THEN the headlines (not before).

**Why:** The body reveals things (angles, benefits, mechanisms — inspiration for headlines comes FROM the body content, not before it).

Write the body first → Extract headlines (10-20 variations based on the body's angles) → Test → Winner.

**Not:** Headline first, then force the body to match the headline (this is reversed — it constrains the body and limits it).
