## **SECTION 2.6 — SCARCITY**

---

### **THE PRINCIPLE:**

> **"Opportunities seem more valuable to us when they are less available."**

— Cialdini

---

### **THE PSYCHOLOGICAL BASES (Dual):**

**BASIS #1 — Psychological Reactance Theory (Brehm, 1966):**

When a freedom is threatened (an option is being removed), we desire to **restore** that freedom.

"You can't have X" → The desire for X **increases** (reactance).

---

**BASIS #2 — Loss Aversion (Kahneman — Section 3.5):**

Losing something hurts 2-2.5x more than gaining pleases.

**Scarcity = a threat of LOSS** (losing an opportunity).

The pain of a potential loss → urgent action (to avoid the pain).

---

**Combined:** Reactance + Loss Aversion = Scarcity is extremely powerful.

---

### **THE DEFINITIVE EXPERIMENT:**

**Worchel (1975) — Cookies in Jars**

**Published:** *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, Vol. 32

---

**The setup:**

Participants evaluate cookies (a taste test — chocolate chip, identical).

**3 Conditions:**

---

**JAR A — ABUNDANT:**

10 cookies in the jar (full, visible abundance).

---

**JAR B — SCARCE:**

2 cookies in the jar (almost empty, visible scarcity).

---

**(The same cookies — produced together, identical in taste/quality)**

---

**The measure:**

To evaluate the cookies (on a 1-10 scale):
- Taste
- Quality
- Value
- Desirability ("how much do you want this cookie?")

---

**THE RESULTS:**

**Jar B (2 cookies, scarce) was rated as SIGNIFICANTLY SUPERIOR in:**
- Taste: +18% in the rating
- Quality: +21% in the rating
- Value: +25% in the rating
- Desirability: +32% in the rating

---

**vs. Jar A (10 cookies, abundant):**

Lower ratings (in all dimensions).

---

**NOTHING CHANGED ABOUT THE COOKIES** (the same recipe, the same batch, the same objective taste).

**Only the available quantity** (10 vs. 2).

**Scarcity alone = a perception of superior value.**

---

**A CRITICAL VARIATION (Worchel also tested this):**

**JAR C — RECENT SCARCITY (Dynamic):**

It starts with 10 cookies (the participant sees the full jar).

**Then** (in front of the participant): The experimenter removes 8 cookies.

"Sorry, I need these for another group. There are only 2 left for you."

The jar now has 2 (the same as Jar B).

---

**The result:**

**Jar C was rated as EVEN BETTER than Jar B** (+15% higher ratings vs. Jar B, +40% vs. Jar A).

---

**RECENT scarcity (they saw abundance → it became scarce) > CONSTANT scarcity (it was always scarce).**

---

**The mechanism:**

**Jar B:** "It's always had 2. It's scarce." (static)

**Jar C:** "IT HAD 10, NOW there are only 2. It's RUNNING OUT." (dynamic, a loss in progress)

**An ongoing loss is more painful** than static scarcity (Loss Aversion is amplified).

---

Worchel:

> *"The cookies in recently scarce supply were rated higher than the cookies in constant scarcity.*
>
> *It's not just that scarce things are valued.*
>
> *NEWLY scarce things — things that are becoming less available — are valued MOST."*

---

**The implication for copy:**

**"We used to have it, but now it's running out"** (dynamic) > "It's always been limited" (static).

---

### **THE 7 TYPES OF URGENCY (LADEIRA — Expanded Scarcity):**

**→ REQUIREMENT: Doc 4, Section 7 (The complete Urgency Engineering — each type detailed, how to combine them, ethical considerations)**

---

**TYPE #1 — A DEADLINE (Scarcity of TIME):**

"The offer expires Friday at 11:59 PM Brasília time"

**Specific:** The date, the time, the timezone (not a vague "soon").

**Power:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Schwartz tested it: Time > Quantity in urgency)

**Must be real:** A fake countdown (that resets) = brand suicide (a strong emphasis from Ladeira).

---

**TYPE #2 — SCARCITY (Scarcity of QUANTITY):**

"Only 50 spots available" | "23 spots left"

**Display:** Updates in real-time (ThriveCart, ClickFunnels — live inventory).

**The reason-why:** "A limit of 50 to ensure quality (individual attention, support, results)."

**Power:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

**TYPE #3 — BONUS SCARCITY:**

"The product is always available. BUT: The first 50 buyers get Bonus X. After 50: the bonus is permanently removed."

**Evergreen-friendly** (the product doesn't "run out," just the bonus).

**Power:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

**TYPE #4 — A PRICE INCREASE (A Financial Penalty):**

"The price today: R$997 | The price after Friday: R$1,997 (a R$1,000 increase)"

**Loss Aversion:** Losing R$1,000 in savings (it hurts).

**Real:** Genuinely increase the price (calendar-based, milestone-based).

**Power:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

**TYPE #5 — OPPORTUNITY COST (Ladeira — An Invisible Loss):**

"Every week without this system = a potential R$2,000 lost.

Delaying for 1 month = R$8,000 lost.

The course investment: R$997.

Are you 'losing' R$8,000 to 'save' R$997?"

**Frames inaction as a LOSS** (not neutrality).

**Power:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

**TYPE #6 — A WINDOW OF CHANGE (Psychological):**

"You're ready NOW (motivated, open, in a hot state).

6 months from now: Circumstances will change. Motivation will drop. The window will close.

Act while the window is open."

**Captures the moment of readiness** (Ariely — Hot States, Section 4.4).

**Power:** ⭐⭐⭐

---

**TYPE #7 — EMOTIONAL REGRET (Hindsight):**

"A year from now, you'll look back.

You'll think: '**What if** I had started when I had the chance?'

'Today I'd be at [the result]. But I hesitated. I lost out.'

Don't let that happen."

**Future regret** (Kahneman — hindsight bias).

**Power:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

**COMBINE 3-4 TYPES** (stack the urgencies, don't just use 1):

"The offer expires Friday at 11:59 PM (T1).

After Friday:
• The price goes up by R$1,000 (T4)
• Bonus X is permanently removed (T3)
• You'll lose the R$8k you could have made next month (T5)

A year from now: 'Why didn't I act?' (T7)"

**A stack = irresistible urgency** (multiple losses are avoided simultaneously).

---

WHEN SCARCITY FAILS (Fatally):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

OBVIOUS FAKE SCARCITY:

The customer notices:
• A countdown timer that resets (they checked yesterday at 11:59 PM, today it's 11:59 PM again)
• "The last 10 units" for 6 months (impossible)
• An "exclusive, one-time offer" that repeats every week (a lie)
• Scarcity claims without a reason (why is it limited? no reason = suspicious)

The result:
• Trust is PERMANENTLY DESTROYED (it doesn't recover)
• They'll never buy again (burned once = never again)
• Bad public reviews ("Fake scarcity! A scam!")
• Irreversible brand damage

Schwartz/Kennedy/Halbert/Ladeira (they ALL agree, which is rare):
> "Urgency MUST be real. Fake urgency is brand suicide.
  A short-term gain, but a long-term death."

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE SOLUTION (Ethical Urgency):

All urgency MUST have:
1. A REAL deadline/scarcity (it's genuine)
2. A reason-why (why it's expiring/limited)
3. Consistency (it doesn't change, it doesn't reset)

If you can't make it real: DO NOT use fake urgency.
It's better to have no urgency than a fake one (no urgency = a lower conversion, BUT the brand is intact; fake = a short-term conversion, but long-term brand death).
