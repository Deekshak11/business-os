## **SECTION 1.2 — TYPE #1: THE DISCOVERY**

---

### **DEFINITION & FORMULA:**

**The Discovery Big Idea** (the most common type for health/science/research-backed products):

**Base formula:**
```
"[Authority: Scientists/Experts/Institution] discovered/revealed/found [X: a finding/mechanism/compound] that [Benefit: the specific result the customer achieves]"
```

**Central angle:** A scientific or authoritative discovery (new knowledge, breakthrough research, a recently published study).

**Source of credibility:** Science/institutional authority (it's not "I think" or "trust me" — it's "Harvard proved," "Published in JAMA," "1,200 participants tested").

---

**Logical structure:**

1.  **Authority** (a credible institution/researcher) gives it weight
2.  **Discovery** (found/revealed) implies it's new (not old news)
3.  **Finding** (a specific mechanism/compound) = tangible
4.  **Benefit** (a specific result) = a clear outcome

**Combined:** Authority discovered a specific thing that delivers a specific benefit = **a credible promise** (not an empty one).

---

**Why it works (Psychological mechanisms — validation from Doc 1):**

**A. Authority** (Cialdini, Doc 1 Section 2.5):

Milgram validated: 65% of people will obey an authority figure even to the point of administering a "lethal" shock.

**Institutional authority** (Harvard, MIT, Mayo Clinic) = **automatic obedience** (trust without verifying — a System 1 heuristic: "If Harvard said it = it must be true").

---

**B. Novelty** (the word "discovery"):

"Discovered" implies: It didn't exist before (or we didn't know about it), and NOW we do (it's recent).

**Novelty = attention** (System 1's orientation to novelty — an evolutionary advantage to detect changes in the environment).

---

**C. Science = Logic** (System 2 can endorse it):

Even though System 1 decides (emotionally), the lazy System 2 likes it when there's a "reason" (Doc 1 Section 1.3 — Langer's "because").

Science provides the reason ("it works because of [a scientific mechanism]").

S2: "OK, that makes sense" (endorsement) → S1's decision proceeds (without S2's resistance).

---

**When it works BEST (Ideal contexts):**

✅ **Health/Wellness products:**

A natural fit (the human body is a biological system, science explains it, studies are abundant).

Examples: Supplements (ingredient research), fitness (exercise science), sleep (circadian studies), nutrition (metabolic research).

---

✅ **Science-based products (any category):**

Tech with research (AI algorithms, efficiency studies), validated methodologies (pedagogy research, learning science), data-backed processes (tested business systems, ROI studies).

---

✅ **B2B (Decision committees, analytical buyers):**

B2B buyers value research/data (not just testimonials — they want hard evidence, studies, white papers).

The discovery angle = speaks their language (rational, evidence-based).

---

✅ **Skeptical audiences (Stage 4-5, have been burned before):**

High cynicism (promises don't work, they've tried everything).

**Authority/science = a higher credibility threshold** than marketing claims alone.

"A Harvard study" > "trust me, bro" (infinitely more credible to skeptics).

---

✅ **You HAVE a real study/research:**

A peer-reviewed journal is ideal (JAMA, NEJM, Nature, Science).

An institutional study is the minimum (a Harvard, MIT, or Stanford press release).

A credible publication is acceptable (reputable health/business journals).

**Criticality:** The study must be REAL (citable, findable, verifiable) — a fake study = fraud/destruction.

---

**Validation self-audit:**

Before using the Discovery type:

☐ Does the study exist? (Can you provide a citation: Journal, Vol, Year, Pages)

☐ Is the study credible? (Peer-reviewed OR an institutional press release, n>50 participants minimum)

☐ Does the study relate to the product? (The connection is logical, not forced)

☐ Are you representing the findings accurately? (Not exaggerating, not distorting)

**IF 4/4 ✅:** The Discovery Big Idea is viable (proceed).

**IF <4:** Do not use the Discovery type (find a different type — Conspiracy, Mistake, Shortcut).

---

### **THE DISCOVERY CREATION PROCESS (4 Specific Steps):**

---

**STEP 1 — Identify the Core Benefit (Same for all types):**

What does the product deliver (the transformational outcome)?

Example: "Lose weight permanently (not temporarily)" or "Earn R$10k-30k/month as a freelance copywriter."

**Time:** 15 minutes (a clarity exercise).

---

**STEP 2 — Find RELATED Research/Study:**

**Critical:** The study does NOT need to be ABOUT your product specifically (your product might be new, from a small company, with no budget for its own research).

**It needs to be:** A study about the **MECHANISM**, **INGREDIENT**, **CATEGORY**, or **RELATED CONCEPT** that your product uses/implements.

---

**Research sources:**

**A. Google Scholar** (`scholar.google.com`):

Search: `"[ingredient OR mechanism OR benefit] study"` or `"[topic] research"`

Filter: Since 2015 (the last 10 years — makes "a recent discovery" credible)

Example: A product has green tea extract → Search "EGCG metabolism study" → Finds a 2019 UCLA research paper showing EGCG increases fat oxidation by 17%.

---

**B. PubMed** (`pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov`):

Health/medical studies (NIH database, free, credible).

Search: MeSH terms (medical subject headings) or keywords.

Example: "chromium picolinate insulin sensitivity" → 200+ studies (choose a recent one, with a large n, from a credible journal).

---

**C. University Press Releases:**

MIT News, Stanford News, Harvard Gazette, etc.

Breakthroughs are announced (in accessible language, written by journalists — easier to adapt for copy than a dense journal paper).

Example: MIT News search "productivity" → Finds a press release about an algorithm study.

---

**D. Industry Journals/Publications:**

Trade journals (Harvard Business Review, Journal of Marketing, etc.).

Studies are cited, with interpretations (they bridge the academic → practical).

---

**Connection strategy (if the study isn't exactly about your product):**

**Your product:** A weight loss supplement (contains green tea extract + chromium + CLA).

**Study found:** A UCLA study on EGCG (a component of green tea) showing it increases fat oxidation.

**The connection:** "Our product contains green tea extract (a source of EGCG). A UCLA study (2019, 340 participants) showed that EGCG increases fat oxidation by 17%. Therefore: Our product delivers this benefit (via the scientifically validated EGCG)."

**The logical bridge:** The ingredient in your product → A study about that ingredient → The benefit the study showed = the benefit your product delivers.

---

**Tangential is OK** (it doesn't have to be direct):

A study on a general mechanism (e.g., ketosis) + your product induces that mechanism (a keto diet) = a valid connection.

A study on a related benefit (e.g., inflammation reduction) + your mechanism affects inflammation (an anti-inflammatory ingredient) = the connection works.

**The criterion:** The connection must make logical SENSE (not be random/forced).

---

**STEP 3 — Frame It as a "Discovery" (An Intriguing Presentation):**

**Not enough** (dry, academic):

"A study shows that EGCG increases fat oxidation by 17%." (factual but boring, no narrative hook)

---

**Enough** (a discovery narrative):

"UCLA Scientists DISCOVER: A Green Tea Compound (EGCG) Burns Fat 17% Faster — Even At Rest (Study of 340 Overweight Adults, 12 Weeks, Published in the Obesity Journal, 2019)"

---

**Transformation elements:**

"A study shows" → "Scientists DISCOVER" (discovery = exciting, a narrative, not a dry report)

"EGCG" → "A Green Tea Compound (EGCG)" (accessible — green tea is familiar, EGCG is scientific)

"Increases oxidation by 17%" → "Burns Fat 17% Faster — Even At Rest" (benefit translation, "even at rest" = an effortless angle)

Stats alone → Stats + context (340 adults, 12 weeks, Obesity Journal 2019 — layers of credibility)

---

**Framing words** (for the Discovery type):

- "Discover" (not "find" — discovery = bigger, has a breakthrough feel)
- "Reveal" (unveiling, hidden before)
- "A new study shows" (recency)
- "Breakthrough" (innovation, advancement)
- "Scientists finally" (implies a long search, now answered)

**Avoid:**
- "A study says" (weak, passive)
- "Research indicates" (academic, dry)
- "Data shows" (factual but no narrative)

**Discovery = a STORY** (scientists searching, finding, revealing — narrative > facts alone).

---

**STEP 4 — Add Proof Elements (A Credibility Boost):**

Layer additional validation (don't just mention the study — develop its credibility):

---

**Element A — Participant Numbers:**

"A study of 340 adults" → A specific n (a large sample = credible, not anecdotal).

Threshold: n>100 is ideal, n>50 is acceptable, n<50 is questionable (mention it if >100, omit it if <50).

---

**Element B — Results Percentage:**

"73% lost 15+ lbs" → The majority (not an outlier), a specific outcome (15+ lbs), and honest (73% not 100% — variance is realistic).

---

**Element C — Timeframe:**

"in 12 weeks" → A reasonable duration (not a "7 days" miracle — believable).

---

**Element D — Institution Name:**

"UCLA" (the institution's brand = instant credibility, not "The University of [Unknown]").

Top-tier institutions (use when available): Harvard, MIT, Stanford, Johns Hopkins, Mayo Clinic, Yale, Columbia, Cambridge, Oxford.

---

**Element E — Journal Citation:**

"Published in the Obesity Journal, 2019" → Peer-reviewed (credibility), recent (2019 = within 5 years), and a relevant journal (Obesity = on-topic).

Ideal: Top-tier journals (JAMA, NEJM, Nature, Science, The Lancet).

Acceptable: Reputable specialty journals.

Avoid: Obscure/predatory journals (low credibility).

---

**Element F — Specific Mechanism:**

"EGCG increases fat oxidation" → HOW it works (not just "it works" — a mechanism = logic).

---

**Combining ALL elements:**

**A COMPLETE Discovery Big Idea (with layered proof):**

"UCLA Scientists Discover a Green Tea Compound (EGCG) Burns Fat 17% Faster Even At Rest — A Study of 340 Overweight Adults (12 Weeks), Published in the International Journal of Obesity in 2019, Showed Results: 73% Lost 15+ Lbs, 89% Specifically Reduced Belly Fat (Visceral — The Hardest Type), The Mechanism: EGCG Increases Fat Oxidation Without Requiring Exercise"

**Proof layers:**
- Authority: UCLA (a tier-1 institution)
- Compound: EGCG specifically (not a generic "green tea")
- Benefit: 17% faster, even at rest (effortless)
- Sample: 340 adults (a large n)
- Duration: 12 weeks (reasonable)
- Journal: Int. J. Obesity 2019 (peer-reviewed, recent, credible)
- Results: 73% lost 15+ lbs (majority, specific), 89% lost belly fat (specificity — the most desired outcome)
- Mechanism: Increases oxidation (HOW is explained)
- No exercise: Removes a barrier (accessibility)

**9 proof elements** (vs. "scientists discover a compound that burns fat" = 1 element, weak).

**Layering = maximum credibility.**

---

### **DISCOVERY BIG IDEA VARIATIONS:**

---

**VARIATION A — The Institution Discovery (Most Common, Strongest):**

The authority = the institution's name (Harvard, MIT, etc.), not a person.

**Examples:**

a) "Harvard Medical School Discovers a Mineral Deficiency Causes 67% of High Blood Pressure Cases (Not Salt, as Was Believed for 50 Years) — a 3-Food Fix Corrects It, Tested on 1,890 Patients"

→ Authority (Harvard Med), an inversion (not salt — a blend with the Mistake element), specific (a 3-food fix), proof (1,890)

b) "MIT Engineers Develop an Algorithm That Eliminates 73% of Busy Work (Tested on 1,200 Knowledge Workers, 4.7 Hours Freed Up Daily, Productivity +68%, Zero Tasks Missed)"

→ Authority (MIT), benefit (73% of busy work gone), proof (1,200 workers, 4.7h specifically, productivity measured), reassurance (zero missed)

---

**When to use an Institution:**

✅ An institutional study exists (a press release or paper is accessible)

✅ The institution is top-tier (brand recognition — Harvard, MIT vs. "The University of [Unknown]")

✅ For a mass audience (an institution's name = instant credibility, no need to explain credentials)

---

**VARIATION B — The Researcher Discovery (Personal Authority):**

The authority = a person (Dr. Name, a specific researcher), not the institution alone.

**Example:**

"Dr. Sarah Chen, a Cardiologist at Johns Hopkins for 30 Years with 12,000+ Patients, Discovers: Chest Pain in 40% of Cases is NOT the Heart (It's Costochondritis — Inflammation of the Rib Cartilage, Treatable in 7 Days Without Pills, BUT 90% of Doctors Misdiagnose It as a Cardiac Issue)"

→ Personal authority (Dr. Chen, credentials: Johns Hopkins, 30 years, 12k patients), discovery (40% misdiagnosed — shocking), benefit (treatable in 7 days), a twist (not the heart, it's cartilage), a problem (90% of doctors get it wrong — a blend with the Mistake element)

---

**When to use a Researcher:**

✅ The researcher has strong credentials + an interesting story (the journey of discovery, a narrative of persistence)

✅ A personal connection is possible (Dr. Chen could do a video, an endorsement, an interview — a human face)

✅ For a niche audience (they might recognize the researcher's name — the community is small, Dr. Chen is known)

**Disadvantage:** A person is less universally known than an institution (you need to establish their credentials inline — "Johns Hopkins, 30 years," etc.).

---

**VARIATION C — The Ancient Rediscovery (Tradition + Modern Science):**

Combine traditional knowledge (centuries/millennia) + modern validation (a recent study).

**Formula:**
```
"Scientists Rediscover an Ancient [Technique/Remedy] (Used in [Time Period]) — A Modern Study Validates: [Benefit] ([Data])"
```

**Examples:**

a) "Scientists Rediscover an Ancient Japanese Longevity Practice (Used for 1,000+ Years by Okinawan Centenarians) — A 25-Year Harvard Study Validates: It Adds an Average of 7.3 Years to Lifespan (Tracked 11,000 Participants)"

→ Tradition (1,000 years, Okinawan centenarians — credible longevity), modern validation (a 25-year Harvard study, 11k participants — a massive study), benefit (7.3 years specifically)

b) "An Ayurvedic Herb (Used for 3,000 Years in India) Rediscovered by Johns Hopkins — Reduces Inflammation by 43% (A Study of 670 Arthritis Patients, 16 Weeks, Published in Arthritis & Rheumatology, 2021)"

→ Ancient (3,000 years, Ayurveda — credibility of tradition), rediscovered (by Johns Hopkins — a modern authority), benefit (43% less inflammation, 670 patients, peer-reviewed)

---

**When to use Ancient Rediscovery:**

✅ A traditional practice/remedy exists (with a documented history — Ayurveda, TCM, indigenous medicine)

✅ A modern study has validated it (in the last 5-10 years, peer-reviewed)

✅ The audience is receptive to tradition (some distrust "new" pharma, preferring "time-tested" methods)

**The appeal:** Double credibility (tradition = centuries of testing, science = modern validation) — the best of both worlds.

---

**VARIATION D — The Industry Insider Discovery (Access-Based, Not Science):**

Authority via insider access/position (not research), a "secret" discovered through inside knowledge.

**Formula:**
```
"A(n) [Industry] Insider [Position] Reveals a [Secret/Strategy] (Used Internally, Never Public) That [Benefit]"
```

**Examples:**

a) "An Ex-Goldman Sachs VP Reveals the 3-Fund Portfolio Strategy (Used Internally at Goldman to Manage $2 Trillion, Never Shared with the Public) — It Beats the S&P 83% of the Time Over the Last 20 Years, Rebalance Once a Quarter (10 Mins of Work)"

→ Insider (ex-Goldman VP — a credible position), a secret (for internal use, not public), scale ($2T managed — serious), benefit (beats the S&P 83% of the time, minimal work once a quarter)

b) "An Ex-Google Product Manager Leaks the Productivity System (Used by Top Performers at Google, Facebook, and Apple) — A 4-Hour Workday Delivers the Same Output as an 8-Hour Day (Tested on 89 Employees for 6 Months, Productivity Maintained/Increased)"

→ Insider (ex-Google PM), exclusivity (used by top performers at tech giants), benefit (4h = 8h of output), proof (tested on 89 employees for 6 months)

---

**When to use an Insider:**

✅ An industry where "insider knowledge" is plausible (finance, tech, competitive industries)

✅ You HAVE an insider (an ex-employee, a consultant, someone with real access)

✅ Stage 4-5 (receptive to conspiracy — "they don't want you to know")

**Caution:** NDA violations (legal risk — ensure the information is in the public domain or properly disclosed).

---

### **WHEN TO USE THE DISCOVERY BIG IDEA:**

**Context checklist (more checks = a stronger indication):**

✅ **A health/wellness product** (science is a natural fit, studies exist)

✅ **An ingredient/mechanism is research-backed** (a study on a component exists)

✅ **A B2B audience** (they value data, research, ROI studies)

✅ **A skeptical market** (Stage 4-5 — they've been burned, need a higher standard of authority/proof)

✅ **Recent research exists** (<5-10 years old — makes a "new discovery" credible)

✅ **The competition uses generic claims** (everyone says "it works," no one cites science — you can differentiate via authority)

---

**When it's LESS ideal (consider other types):**

⚠️ **A product without scientific backing:**

If zero studies are related (even tangentially) → a forced Discovery is a fake one.

**Do not invent** a study (that's fraud).

Better to use: a Shortcut (easier/faster doesn't need a study), a Conspiracy (if the industry fits), or a Story (testimonial-heavy).

---

⚠️ **A mass consumer audience that doesn't value research:**

Some niches prefer a story > data (emotional buyers, romance/relationships, entertainment, impulse purchases).

The Discovery type works BUT it might not be the strongest (test it vs. Story/Shortcut types).

---

### **DISCOVERY PITFALLS (Avoid Fatal Errors):**

---

**PITFALL #1 — The Study Doesn't Exist (Total Fabrication):**

Citing "a 2020 Harvard study proves X" when the study does NOT exist = **FRAUD**.

**Legally:** False advertising (an FTC violation, fines, legal action)

**Ethically:** Deliberate deception (a moral violation)

**Practically:** It's discovered FAST (skeptics will Google "Harvard study 2020 [topic]," won't find it, and will expose it publicly — viral backlash, brand destroyed overnight)

**The consequence:** Permanent brand death, forced refunds, legal liability, irreparable reputation.

**NEVER EVER** fabricate a study.

**Validation:** BEFORE citing it, verify the study exists (Google Scholar, PubMed, the institution's site — find the actual paper or press release, save the PDF/link, so you can cite it if questioned).

---

**PITFALL #2 — The Study Exists BUT Is Misrepresented (Distortion):**

The real study finding: "Ingredient X may support weight management when combined with a caloric deficit and exercise" (a modest, qualified, cautious scientific language).

Your claim: "Ingredient X CURES obesity! Guaranteed weight loss without diet or exercise!" (an extreme exaggeration — "may support" ≠ "cures," "with diet/exercise" was omitted).

**Verification:** A skeptic reads the original study (publicly available in a journal or on Sci-Hub), sees the massive discrepancy, and exposes the distortion.

**The consequence:** Zero credibility ("they lied about the study, what else are they lying about?"), trust is destroyed, legal risk (misrepresentation).

---

**The rule:** Represent findings **ACCURATELY**.

You can: Simplify the language (scientific jargon → accessible), and emphasize relevant aspects (focus on the findings that support your product).

You cannot: Exaggerate the numbers (17% → 170%), omit qualifiers ("when combined with X" = important context), reverse causation, or cherry-pick (ignore contradicting findings).

**Honesty = long-term** (the short-term gains of lying < the long-term trust of honesty).

---

**PITFALL #3 — The Study Is Real BUT Weak/Low-Credibility:**

The study exists BUT:

❌ n=5-10 participants (too small, not statistically significant)

❌ Not peer-reviewed (a blog post, a company-funded white paper, not independent)

❌ Flawed methodology (no control group, only self-reported data, obvious conflicts of interest not disclosed)

❌ Published in a predatory journal (pay-to-publish journals, fake peer-review — a list exists online, check it)

❌ Outdated (40+ years old, science has evolved since)

---

**Citing a weak study as "proof":** Is WORSE than not citing a study at all (credibility damage when the weakness is exposed).

**Threshold standards:**

✅ n>50 (acceptable), n>100 (good), n>500 (excellent)

✅ Peer-reviewed OR an institutional press release (credible vetting)

✅ Recent (<10 years, <5 is ideal)

✅ Sound methodology (an RCT is ideal, a control group is the minimum, conflicts disclosed)

✅ A reputable journal/institution (top-tier is ideal, credible is the minimum)

**If a study doesn't meet 4/5 of these standards:** Do not cite it (find a better study, or use a different Big Idea type).

---

**PITFALL #4 — Overclaiming Based on a Study:**

A modest study finding: "Ingredient X was associated with an 8% reduction in symptom severity (p=0.04, small effect size)."

Your claim: "It CURES the condition!" (an 8% reduction ≠ a cure, "associated" ≠ causation, p=0.04 = barely significant).

**Medical/Health is especially sensitive:** FDA regulations, legal definitions ("cure," "treat," "prevent" are regulated claims, they need approval).

**The claim must match the strength of the study:**

Study: "May support" → Claim: "Supports" (OK)

Study: "Reduces by 8%" → Claim: "Reduces moderately" (OK)

Study: "Cure" → Claim: "Cures" (OK if the study actually says it's a cure — which is rare)

**Never:** Upgrade a study's claim (support → cure, reduce → eliminate, associated → causes).

---

**PITFALL #5 — The Study Is Irrelevant (The Connection Is Forced/Random):**

Your product: A productivity app (task management software).

The study cited: "A Harvard meditation study reduces stress by 23%."

The connection claimed: "Our app uses the principles Harvard discovered..." (meditation ≠ software — the connection isn't logical).

**A skeptic:** "How does a task app 'use meditation principles'? It doesn't make sense. It's a random study citation." (confusion, credibility damage).

---

**The connection must be LOGICAL:**

✅ The ingredient in your product → A study about that ingredient (direct)

✅ The mechanism the product uses → A study about that mechanism (direct)

✅ The category your product is in → A study about that category (general but relevant)

✅ A related concept → The study is tangentially related (acceptable if the bridge is logical)

❌ A random study → A forced association (not logical, a transparent attempt to seem scientific)

---

**Validation:** Explain the connection in one sentence. If you can't (it's convoluted, requires mental gymnastics) → the connection is too weak, don't use it.
