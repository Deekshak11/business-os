## **SECTION 2.7 — UNITY**

---

### **THE PRINCIPLE:**

> **"We say yes to those we consider 'one of us'."**

— Cialdini (added in the 2016 edition, *Pre-Suasion*)

---

### **THE DIFFERENCE FROM LIKING (Critical):**

**LIKING (Section 2.4):**
- I **LIKE** you (a positive relationship, affection)
- Separate individuals (me and you, distinct)

**UNITY:**
- You are **ONE OF US** (a shared identity, belonging)
- A shared identity (we, a collective, a tribe)

---

**Unity > Liking** in persuasive power (Cialdini's 2016 research).

---

**Why:**

Liking = to like (it can change, it's sometimes superficial).

Unity = **identity** (deep, tribal, hard to change).

"I like you" < "You are from my tribe" (biological, ancestral).

---

### **THE BASES OF UNITY (4 Categories):**

---

**BASIS #1 — KINSHIP:**

Family (blood, marriage, adoption).

**Stronger than friendship** (shared genes or legally bound).

---

**BASIS #2 — PLACE:**

The same city, region, country.

"We from São Paulo," "Fellow New Yorkers," "Brazilians."

---

**BASIS #3 — GROUP MEMBERSHIP:**

A sports team, a company, a religion, a political party, alumni.

A collective identity (an in-group).

---

**BASIS #4 — A SHARED EXPERIENCE:**

War veterans (combat bonding).

University alumni (a shared formative experience).

Trauma survivors (mutual suffering).

**An intense shared experience** = deep unity.

---

### **THE EXPERIMENTS:**

---

**"We" vs. "I" (In a Negotiation — Perdue et al., 2008):**

Negotiators were instructed to use the language:

**Group A:** "I think...", "My position is...", "I propose..."

**Group B:** "We can...", "Our solution is...", "We think..."

---

**The result:**

Group B (the "We" language): **40% more successful agreements** (win-win outcomes).

"We" = perceived cooperation (unity) → less adversarial → more mutual concessions.

---

**Arbitrary Uniforms (Platow et al., 1999):**

Participants are given t-shirts (random colors — red or blue, randomized).

Cooperation tasks (they need to work together).

---

**The result:**

People with the **same color** (the same arbitrary uniform):
- Trust each other more
- Cooperate more
- Rate each other as more competent

**vs. different colors** (less trust, less cooperation).

---

**Even though the uniform was ARBITRARY** (distributed randomly, with zero real meaning).

**A symbol of unity > reality.**

---

**Physical Synchronization (Wiltermuth & Heath, 2009):**

Participants **march in sync** (in-step, coordinated) vs. walking in a disorganized way.

**Afterward:** Cooperation tasks.

---

**The result:**

The synchronized group was **much more likely to cooperate** (to share resources, to help).

**Physical synchronization → a feeling of unity** (coordinated movement = "we are one").

---

**An ancestral application:**

Tribal rituals (synchronized dancing, chanting).

The military (marching in-step — for unit cohesion).

Religions (prayers/chants in unison — for congregation unity).

---

**They all use synchronization** to create unity (it has worked for millennia).

---
