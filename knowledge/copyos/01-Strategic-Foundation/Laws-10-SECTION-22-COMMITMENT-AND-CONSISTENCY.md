## **SECTION 2.2 — COMMITMENT AND CONSISTENCY**

---

### **THE PRINCIPLE:**

> **"Once we make a choice or take a stand, we will encounter personal and interpersonal pressures to behave consistently with that commitment."**

— Robert Cialdini

---

### **PSYCHOLOGICAL BASIS (Why This Shortcut Exists):**

**Consistency is socially valued** (in all documented human cultures):

**A consistent person = virtues:**
- Reliable (their word has value)
- Has integrity (consistent principles)
- Strong (doesn't change with the wind)
- Predictable (others can count on you)

**An inconsistent person = vices:**
- A hypocrite (says X, does Y)
- Unstable (changes their mind constantly)
- Weak (no convictions)
- Unreliable (their word is worthless)

---

**Pressures to maintain consistency:**

**INTERNAL pressure (Psychological):**

"I said/did X yesterday.

If today I do Y (the opposite), I'm a **hypocrite to myself**.

My self-image (self-concept) is to be a consistent, integral person.

Inconsistency threatens my self-image (intense psychological discomfort — cognitive dissonance).

Therefore: I MUST continue to do X (or admit I was wrong — the ego doesn't accept that easily)."

---

**EXTERNAL pressure (Social):**

"So-and-so saw me publicly say X yesterday.

If today I do Y (the opposite), So-and-so will judge me: 'A hypocrite. Their word is worthless.'

My social reputation is to be a person of my word.

Public inconsistency destroys my reputation (potential social ostracism).

Therefore: I MUST continue to do X (or suffer social judgment)."

---

**The combined result:**

After a commitment (especially if it's public, written, or effortful):

**HUGE pressure to behave consistently** (even if circumstances change, even if you want to change your mind, even if new information suggests otherwise).

---

Cialdini:

> *"It is, quite simply, our nearly obsessive desire to be (and to appear) consistent with what we have already done.*
>
> *Once we make a choice or take a stand, we encounter personal and interpersonal pressures to behave consistently with that commitment.*
>
> *Those pressures cause us to respond in ways that justify our earlier decision."*

---

### **THE DEFINITIVE EXPERIMENT (Moriarty, 1972):**

**Published:** *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, Vol. 23, No. 3, 1972

**Author:** Thomas Moriarty (the New York beaches study)

**Title:** "The Effect of Commitment on Compliance"

---

**The setup:**

Public beaches (New York City beaches, summer of 1972).

A confederate (a researcher in disguise) spreads a towel on the sand near a sunbather (a randomly chosen stranger).

They place a **portable radio** visibly on the towel (expensive for the time — $40-50, equivalent to ~$300 today).

---

**2 Experimental Conditions:**

---

**CONDITION A — WITH COMMITMENT:**

The confederate (before getting up to "go for a swim"):

Turns to the sunbather (the stranger): **"Excuse me, could you watch my things for a few minutes while I go for a quick swim?"**

---

The sunbather (most of them): "Sure." / "No problem." / "OK."

[A **VERBAL COMMITMENT is established** — the sunbather has explicitly agreed to "watch the things"]

---

The confederate leaves (goes to the water, out of sight).

---

**CONDITION B — WITHOUT COMMITMENT:**

The confederate simply gets up, leaves for the water.

**Without asking for anything.** Without talking to the sunbather.

The sunbather has made no commitment (zero interaction).

---

**AFTERWARD (In Both Conditions — The Test):**

4-5 minutes after the confederate leaves:

**Another confederate** (pretending to be a thief, but obviously fake — not a real criminal) casually approaches.

**Grabs the radio** (visibly, deliberately obvious).

**Starts to walk/run away** with the radio.

---

**The dependent measure:**

Does the sunbather **intervene?** (get up, yell, chase, confront, try to stop them?)

---

**THE RESULTS:**

---

**CONDITION A (With Commitment — to "watch the things"):**

**95% of the sunbathers PHYSICALLY intervened**

The actions (observed):
- They got up immediately
- They yelled: "Hey!", "Stop!", "That's not yours!"
- They ran after the "thief"
- They physically **grabbed** them (in some cases)
- They demanded the radio back
- Some even fought (a brief struggle)

**95% compliance with the commitment** (almost universal).

---

**CONDITION B (Without Commitment):**

**20% intervened**

The majority (80%): Looked, observed, but didn't act (it wasn't their problem, they didn't get involved).

The 20% who did intervene: Hesitant action (yelled from a distance, didn't physically chase).

---

**THE DIFFERENCE:**

95% vs. 20% = **4.75x more intervention**

**Simply by getting a verbal commitment** ("can you watch?").

---

**ANALYSIS (Why It's So Powerful):**

A simple verbal commitment ("OK, I'll watch") →

**An EXTREME consistent action:**
- Confronting a "thief" (personal physical risk — potential violence)
- Chasing them (physical effort, heat, sand)
- Fighting if necessary (in some cases)
- Protecting the property of a **COMPLETE STRANGER** (zero personal relationship, zero personal benefit)

---

**Even though:**
- They didn't know the owner of the radio (a stranger)
- They wouldn't gain anything by intervening (pure altruism)
- There was a real risk (the thief could have reacted violently — some sunbathers reported fear but acted anyway)
- They could have easily ignored it (as 80% in Condition B did)

---

**The commitment ("OK, I'll watch") = a psychological obligation to act consistently.**

**The pressure of consistency > self-preservation** (physical risk was acceptable to keep their word).

---

Moriarty:

> *"Once a commitment is made, even a small verbal one, self-image and social pressure combine to create powerful motivation for consistency.*
>
> *People will go to remarkable lengths — including personal risk — to appear consistent with their commitments."*

---

### **VARIATIONS OF THE PRINCIPLE:**

---

#### **VARIATION A — A Public Commitment > a Private One (An 8x Difference in Rigidity)**

**The experiment (Deutsch & Gerard, 1955):**

**Published:** *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, Vol. 51

---

**The setup:**

Participants estimate the length of lines (an ambiguous task, where subjective judgment is possible).

**After making their initial estimate:**

They are presented with **contrary evidence** (information suggesting their estimate was wrong).

**The measure:** Do they change their opinion? (accept the contrary evidence vs. stick to their original estimate)

---

**CONDITION A — A PRIVATE Commitment:**

The participant **wrote** their estimate on a private piece of paper (no one else saw it, it was confidential).

**Then:** The contrary evidence was presented.

**Result:** **40% changed their opinion** (they accepted the evidence, admitted their mistake, and corrected their estimate).

---

**CONDITION B — A PUBLIC Commitment:**

The participant **declared** their estimate verbally (a group of 5-7 people heard it, they were witnesses).

**Then:** The same contrary evidence.

**Result:** **5% changed their opinion** (they stuck to their original position, they resisted the evidence).

---

**40% vs. 5% = an 8x difference** in flexibility/rigidity.

---

**The mechanism:**

**Private:** Only INTERNAL pressure (self-image — "admitting a mistake to myself" is manageable).

**Public:** INTERNAL + EXTERNAL pressure (social reputation — "admitting a mistake in front of others" is humiliating, a loss of face).

---

**Social pressure >>> internal pressure** (humans are social animals, LF8 — group approval).

---

**The implication:**

**A public commitment is MUCH more rigid** (hard to reverse, even with evidence).

**Use it in copy:** Make the prospect declare their desire/problem PUBLICLY (in a quiz, a survey, a future testimonial).

---

#### **VARIATION B — A WRITTEN Commitment > a Verbal One (1.6x the Retention)**

**The experiment (Health Clubs/Gyms — Multiple Studies, 1980s-2000s):**

New gym members (at sign-up).

**2 Groups:**

---

**GROUP A — VERBAL Goals:**

The staff asks (orally): "What are your fitness goals?"

The member responds verbally: "To lose 20 lbs," "To work out 3x/week," etc.

The staff writes it down (but the member doesn't write).

---

**GROUP B — WRITTEN Goals:**

The staff gives them a piece of paper: "Write your fitness goals here."

The member **writes** them in their own handwriting (the same goals Group A would have said).

The paper stays with the gym (or with the member — a copy, something tangible).

---

**The measure (3 months later):**

The cancellation rate (they quit the gym).

---

**The results:**

**Group A (verbal):** 45% canceled

**Group B (written):** 28% canceled

**28% vs. 45% = 1.6x better retention** (written vs. verbal).

---

**The mechanism:**

**Verbal:** Ephemeral (said and "forgotten" — there's no tangible record).

**Written:** **Tangible** (a document exists, it remains, it can be re-read).

---

**Tangibility increases commitment:**
- "It exists in the world" (not just in memory)
- It can be shown (to yourself, to others)
- It's harder to deny ("I wrote this" vs. "I said that" — what's written is proof)

---

Cialdini:

> *"Commitments are most effective when they are active, public, and effortful.*
>
> *Writing requires all three: It's active (you do it), it can be made public (you can show others), and it's effortful (more than just speaking)."*

---

**Implication for copy:**

Make the prospect **WRITE** (text input) > just click (a checkbox).

Writing is a stronger commitment (Tactic #3 below).

---

#### **VARIATION C — EFFORT JUSTIFIES COMMITMENT (Cognitive Dissonance)**

**The experiment (Aronson & Mills, 1959 — The Hazing Study):**

**Published:** *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, Vol. 59

---

**The setup:**

University women want to join a "discussion group" on the psychology of sex (an interesting, desirable topic).

**To join:** A "screening test" (hazing).

---

**2 Groups (randomized):**

**GROUP A — SEVERE Hazing:**

The "screening" = reading aloud (in front of a male experimenter):
- Explicit passages from erotic literature (vivid, sexually graphic)
- A list of obscene words (embarrassing, uncomfortable)

**Duration:** 10-15 minutes of extreme embarrassment.

---

**GROUP B — MILD Hazing:**

The "screening" = reading words related to sex (clinical terms, mild).

**Duration:** 2-3 minutes, minimal discomfort.

---

**Both groups were APPROVED** (they joined the group — the hazing was fake, everyone passed).

---

**AFTERWARD:**

They participate in 1 session of the discussion group.

**The reality (an experimental manipulation):**

The session was DELIBERATELY BORING (a trivial topic, a superficial discussion, tedious — designed to be disappointing).

---

**The measure:**

After the session: "How much did you like the group? How interesting was the discussion? How valuable are the members?"

Ratings (on a 1-10 scale).

---

**THE RESULTS (Counter-Intuitive):**

**Group A (SEVERE Hazing):**

The evaluations: Significantly HIGHER

"The group is interesting" (7.2/10)

"The discussion was valuable" (6.8/10)

"I want to continue participating" (85%)

---

**Group B (MILD Hazing):**

The evaluations: LOWER

"The group is boring" (4.1/10)

"The discussion was superficial" (3.9/10)

"I might not come back" (45%)

---

**Group A (who suffered more) evaluated it BETTER** than Group B (who suffered less).

**Even though the group was objectively BORING for both.**

---

**The mechanism (Cognitive Dissonance — Festinger, 1957):**

**Group A's thought process (unconscious):**

"I suffered a LOT to get in (extreme embarrassment, 15 minutes of humiliation).

If the group is worthless, then I suffered for nothing (which would imply I was an idiot, that I made a bad decision).

My ego can't accept that (I'm smart, my decisions are good).

Therefore: The group MUST be worth it (the mind rationalizes to protect the ego, to maintain consistency with the decision to join)."

---

**Group B's thought process:**

"I suffered very little to get in (2-3 min of mild discomfort).

If the group is worthless, that's OK (I lost very little, it's not a big deal).

I can admit: The group is boring (no threat to my ego)."

---

**Festinger/Aronson:**

> *"Persons who go through a great deal of trouble or pain to attain something tend to value it more highly than persons who attain the same thing with minimal effort.*
>
> *This is because they need to JUSTIFY the effort — otherwise they'd feel foolish."*

---

**Effort → Justification → Increased valuation → Deepened commitment.**

---

**The implication (paradoxical):**

**The harder it is to get something = the MORE they value it** (not less).

**Application:** A membership with an "application process" (screening, qualification) is more valued than open-access.

---

#### **VARIATION D — THE FOOT-IN-THE-DOOR (Small → Large)**

**The principle:**

An initial **SMALL** commitment (trivial, easy to accept) → a **LARGE** commitment later (accepted due to consistency with the small one).

---

**The Classic Experiment (Freedman & Fraser, 1966):**

**Published:** *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, Vol. 4

---

**The setup:**

Residences in a California suburb.

**The final request (the same for everyone):** To put a HUGE sign on their front lawn.

The sign: "DRIVE CAREFULLY" (10 feet high, ugly, invasive, blocked the view of the house).

---

**2 Conditions:**

---

**CONDITION A — THE DIRECT REQUEST (The Control):**

A door-to-door approach (cold):

"Would you put this sign on your lawn to promote traffic safety?"

[They show the huge sign — obviously invasive]

---

**Acceptance:** **17%** (a minority — most refuse an ugly/invasive sign)

---

**CONDITION B — THE FOOT-IN-THE-DOOR (Experimental):**

**2-3 WEEKS BEFORE:**

An approach (the same houses, the same person if possible):

"Would you sign this petition about traffic safety?

We're collecting signatures to send to the mayor, asking for more 'Slow Down' signs in residential neighborhoods."

[A piece of paper, a pen, 30 seconds to sign — trivial, a good cause, zero cost]

---

**A small commitment:** 90%+ signed (easy, fast, a reasonable cause).

---

**2-3 weeks LATER (the same person who signed):**

An approach (now the large request):

"Would you put this sign on your lawn?" [The same huge sign]

---

**Acceptance:** **76%**

---

**76% vs. 17% = 4.47x more compliance** (almost 5x).

**Simply by getting a micro-commitment first** (signing a petition).

---

**THE MECHANISM (Self-Labeling + Consistency):**

**Signing the petition** (a small action) →

**A self-label is formed** (unconsciously):

"I'm a person who cares about traffic safety.

I'm a responsible citizen, active in social causes."

**An identity is adopted** (the self-concept is slightly modified).

---

**Weeks later, the request for the sign:**

Refusing = **inconsistency** with the self-label:

"I said (via my signature) that I care about safety.

But now I refuse to really help (with the sign)?

That's hypocritical. Did my action (signing) have no meaning? Am I a poser?"

**Ego/reputation (the self-concept) is threatened by a refusal.**

---

**Accepting = consistency** with the self-label:

"I'm a person committed to safety (I signed the petition).

Now I'm demonstrating that commitment (I'm accepting the sign, even if it's invasive).

My actions are consistent. I have integrity."

---

**Consistency with self-image > the inconvenience of the sign.**

---

Freedman & Fraser:

> *"Once a person's self-image is aligned with a position ('I am someone who cares about X'), subsequent requests consistent with that position are much harder to refuse.*
>
> *The initial commitment, even if small, changes self-perception.*
>
> *Later requests are evaluated not just on their merit, but on their consistency with one's self-image."*

---

**The Foot-in-the-Door is powerful because:**

It's not just about the initial action (signing).

It's about a **change of identity** (self-labeling).

An identity is sticky (hard to reverse).

WHEN COMMITMENT FAILS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A new commitment CONTRADICTS a previous one (extreme dissonance).

Example:
A prospect bought Product A (a competitor's) 2 months ago ($500).

You ask: "Buy Product B (yours, similar, $600)."

The prospect: INTENSE resistance.
Why? Buying B = admitting A was a mistake (the ego can't accept it).
"I spent $500 on A 2 months ago. If I now buy B (a replacement), does that mean A was a waste? Did I make a bad decision? Am I stupid? NO."

The result: A refusal (to protect the previous decision, to maintain consistency).

THE SOLUTION:

Don't force a direct contradiction.

Frame it as an "UPGRADE" or a "NEXT STEP" (not a replacement):

"Product A was great to START with (it validates their past choice — it wasn't a mistake).

Product B is the NEXT LEVEL (it doesn't replace A — it builds on it, a natural evolution).

You started with A (a smart move). Now you're ready for B (the next stage)."

Consistency is maintained: A was right AT THE TIME. B is right NOW.
(A progression, not a contradiction.)

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

DEFENSES (When You are the Prospect):
──────────────────────────────────

Cialdini:

"Ask: 'Knowing what I know NOW, would I make the same commitment?'

If NO: You can change (a new decision based on new information — it's not inconsistency, it's intelligent adaptation).

If YES: Keep the commitment (genuine consistency)."

SEPARATE:

A past decision (based on past info, a past context)
vs.
A present decision (current info, a current context)

BLIND consistency = "a foolish consistency, the hobgoblin of little minds" (Emerson).

INFORMED consistency = a virtue.
