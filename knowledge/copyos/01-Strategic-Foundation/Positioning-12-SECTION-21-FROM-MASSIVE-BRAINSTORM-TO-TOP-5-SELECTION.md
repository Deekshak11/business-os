## **SECTION 2.1 — FROM MASSIVE BRAINSTORM → TO TOP 5 SELECTION**

---

### **THE EMPIRICUS PROCESS (20+ Years, R$2+ Billion in Revenue Attributed to Big Ideas):**

Empiricus/Agora (financial newsletters in Brazil/US, info products) have refined their process over 2 decades and hundreds of launches.

**The key insight:** "Initial quantity → final quality" (the more you generate, the better the best one is).

---

**PHASE 1 — A MASSIVE BRAINSTORM:**

**If it's a team:** 30-50 Big Ideas are generated (a 90-120min session, 4-6 people).

**If it's solo:** 15-25 Big Ideas (a 90min session, force the quantity).

**Do not:** Generate just 3-5 ideas (insufficient exploration, you'll settle for something suboptimal).

---

**PHASE 2 — THE FIRST ELIMINATION (Obvious, Quick):**

Automatically remove:

❌ **The impossible:** Ideas that violate physics/biology (not believable, scam-like)

❌ **The off-brand:** Ideas that contradict your brand's values/positioning (they'll alienate your core)

❌ **The offensive:** Racism, sexism, insensitivity (a legal/ethical/PR risk)

❌ **Legal issues:** Illegal health claims ("it cures cancer"), prohibited financial guarantees ("guaranteed returns"), etc.

❌ **Competitor-identical:** The exact same thing a main competitor is already using (it doesn't differentiate, it's a copycat)

**The cut:** ~20-30% (the obviously weak/problematic ones).

**Remaining:** 20-40 Big Ideas (they've passed the first filter).

---

**PHASE 3 — THE INITIAL SCORING (The 6-Criteria Checklist — Next Section):**

For EACH remaining Big Idea (20-40):

Score it on the 6 criteria (Bold, New, Believable, Exclusive, Emotional, Urgent).

**The spreadsheet:**

| Big Idea | B | N | B | E | E | U | Total |
|----------|---|---|---|---|---|---|-------|
| Idea #1  | 1 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 1 | 5     |
| Idea #2  | 1 | 0 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 4     |
| [...]    |   |   |   |   |   |   |       |

**Sort:** Descending by the total (6/6 at the top, 0/6 at the bottom).

**Time:** 30-60min (to rigorously score 20-40 ideas).

---

**PHASE 4 — ELIMINATE THE <4/6s:**

Cut the Big Ideas that scored 0-3/6 (they're weak, not worth refining — your time is better spent on the strong ones).

**Remaining:** Typically 8-15 Big Ideas (scored 4-6/6 — the candidates).

---

**PHASE 5 — SELECT THE TOP 5:**

**If you have 8-15 candidates:**

Choose the top 5 (the highest scores).

**The tiebreaker** (if multiple have the same score, e.g., three 5/6s):

a) **Intuition:** Gut feel (which one is more exciting, the team votes)

b) **Stage fit:** Which type is best for the diagnosed Stage (from Doc 2)

c) **Proof availability:** Which one has more proof ready (studies, data, cases — it's easier to prove)

d) **Mechanism defensibility:** Which mechanism is harder for competitors to copy

---

**Phase 5 output:** 5 Big Ideas (scored 4-6/6, the top candidates).

---

**Why the TOP 5 (not just 1):**

**Reason A — To refine multiple in parallel:**

Don't put all your eggs in one basket (if your favorite idea #1 doesn't refine well, you have backups #2-3 ready).

---

**Reason B — To test 3-5 on the market:**

The winner is often NOT the "internal favorite" (you/the team loves idea #1, BUT the market prefers #3 — constant surprises).

Testing 3-5 is insurance (the market decides, not just you).

---

**Reason C — Backup options:**

If idea #1 performs BUT a competitor launches something similar 2 months later (they copy you) → pivot to the refined idea #2 (it's already ready).

---

**Empiricus:**

> *"Never commit to 1 Big Idea without having refined 3-5 and tested a minimum of 2-3.*
>
> *Surprises: An idea we LOVED (the team's consensus favorite) flopped (a 0.6% CVR).*
>
> *An idea we were 'meh' about (we only included it to diversify the test) WON (a 4.1% CVR — 7x better).*
>
> *The Market > opinions (always).*
>
> *Testing multiple = being humble (admitting you don't know everything, letting the market teach you)."*
