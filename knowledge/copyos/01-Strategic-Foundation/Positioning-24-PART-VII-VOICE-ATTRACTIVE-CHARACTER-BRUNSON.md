## **PART VII — VOICE & ATTRACTIVE CHARACTER (BRUNSON)**

**Words:** 6,400

---

**Section 7.1 — Why Voice Matters (800w):**

People buy from PEOPLE (not from brands/corporations).

Voice = personal differentiation (in commodity markets, personality is a moat).

Brunson: "Build a cult of personality" (a following for a person, not just a product).

Examples: Halbert (profane/direct), Ogilvy (refined/British), Brunson (enthusiastic/stories), Ladeira (didactic/Brazilian) — each unique voice generated a loyal following.

---

**Section 7.2 — The 5 Elements of an Attractive Character (4,000w):**

**Each element is developed with 800 words**

---

**7.2.1 — BACKSTORY (Origin, Struggle, Journey):**

What it is: Your story (where you were, your struggles, how you got here).

Why it matters: Identification (Collier — "I was like you," relatability).

The structure: Before (a relatable struggle) → Obstacles (failed attempts) → The turning point (a discovery/epiphany) → After (the transformation) → Now (the results are maintained, you teach others).

Examples: Brunson's backstory (a broke wrestling coach → $100M with funnels), Halbert (an ex-convict → a top copywriter), are developed.

In the copy: The origin story in a VSL/Webinar (from min 0-10), on the about page, in occasional emails.

---

**7.2.2 — PARABLES (Stories with Lessons):**

What they are: Personal stories that teach principles.

Brunson: "Every email should tell a story → teach a lesson → and tie it to the offer."

The structure: The story (2-3 paragraphs) → The lesson ("Here's what I learned...") → The application ("This is why [the product] works / why you need X").

Examples: 3 complete parable structures (a family story, a business failure story, a client win story).

Frequency: 70-90% of emails (storytelling is dominant, not constant pitching).

---

**7.2.3 — CHARACTER FLAWS (Vulnerability):**

What they are: Admitting your weaknesses, your imperfections (not being perfect/polished).

Why: It humanizes you (relatability — Doc 1 Section 2.4 Liking, vulnerability increases liking).

Master examples: Brunson "I'm terrible at tech," Kennedy "I went bankrupt in the '70s," Halbert "I'm an ex-convict, I've had 13 failures."

**Caution:** The flaw can NOT contradict your core expertise (a copywriter can't say "I don't know how to write" — it destroys their authority).

The flaw must be in a SECONDARY area (tech, organization, social skills), not a primary one (your core competence).

Guidelines: Which ones to admit, which ones not to, and how to present them (honestly, not with extreme self-deprecation).

---

**7.2.4 — POLARITY (Strong Stances, Enemies):**

What it is: Having strong opinions, declaring yourself FOR something, AGAINST something, and identifying "enemies" (concepts, systems, not people).

Brunson's examples: PRO-funnels, ANTI-websites ("websites are dead"), PRO-entrepreneurship, ANTI-corporate jobs.

The effect: It polarizes (some will love you, some will hate you) BUT a tribe forms (an in-group with intense loyalty).

Warren, Doc 1 Section 6.0 #5: Throw rocks at your enemies (tribal bonding).

In the copy: Declare your positions (in manifestos, occasional rants, by identifying the enemy).

Examples: 3 polarities are developed (business, health, social).

---

**7.2.5 — THE TWO JOURNEYS (Hero → Guide):**

McKee's (*Story*) structure applied to copywriting.

**Journey #1 — You as the HERO:**

Your journey (you overcame an obstacle, you achieved a result) — to build credibility.

Tell it FIRST (to establish your authority — you've DONE it, it's not just theory).

---

**Journey #2 — You as the GUIDE:**

Now you help OTHERS on the same journey (you're a mentor, a teacher).

The customer becomes the protagonist (the hero of their own story), and you are the guide (Obi-Wan, not Luke).

Tell it AFTER Journey #1 (the transition from hero → guide, the offer is the vehicle the clients use).

The structure is developed, with examples of transitions, and when to emphasize each journey (early on = the hero's credibility, later = the guide's helpfulness).

---

**Section 7.3 — The Voice Spectrum: 4 Dimensions (1,200w):**

**Calibrate your voice on 4 continuums:**

**Dimension #1 — FORMALITY:**
```
Formal ←────────────────→ Casual
(Ogilvy)  (Masterson)  (Halbert)
```

**Dimension #2 — EMOTIONAL TONE:**
```
Rational ←──────────────→ Emotional
(Bly B2B)    (Hybrid)    (Vitale)
```

**Dimension #3 — LANGUAGE:**
```
Polished ←──────────────→ Profane
(Ogilvy)   (Kennedy)    (Halbert)
```

**Dimension #4 — VULNERABILITY:**
```
Polished ←──────────────→ Raw
(Agora)    (Brunson)   (Ladeira)
```

---

Choose a position on EACH dimension based on your avatar (from Doc 2 — their age, their values, their language).

Examples of voices plotted (Ogilvy, Halbert, Brunson, Ladeira — where each one falls on the spectrum), how to choose your position, and how to calibrate it.

---

**Section 7.4 — Consistency Guidelines (400w):**

Your voice MUST be consistent (in emails, ads, pages, social media — everywhere).

Inconsistency is confusing (a shifting personality = distrust).

A consistency checklist, examples of violations (a formal email → a casual ad = jarring), and how to maintain it.

---

**Application Box Part VII (700w):**

A worksheet: Define your voice (the 5 elements + the 4 dimensions), test "is it recognizable without my name?" (your voice is strong if people can identify you without seeing your byline), and a consistency audit.
