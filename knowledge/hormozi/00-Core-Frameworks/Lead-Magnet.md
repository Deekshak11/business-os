# Lead Magnet

## When to use

- Core offer alone doesn’t get enough engaged leads  
- Expensive products need a first “yes”  
- Stage 0 free product design  
- Paid ads need a better conversion asset than “book a call” only  

## Definitions

- **Lead:** a person you can contact  
- **Engaged lead:** a person who shows interest in what you sell  
- **Lead magnet:** a **complete solution to a narrow problem**, usually free or low cost, that reveals another problem solved by your **core offer**

### Pretzel model
Salty pretzels (lead magnet) solve narrow hunger → create thirst → drink (core offer) is sold for money. Pretzels should be good enough to charge for; drink revenue must cover pretzel cost at profit.

## Rules

1. Even free has a **time cost** — if not worth their time, it’s overpriced.  
2. Apply **Grand Slam Offer** standards to free offers.  
3. Person who pays with time now is more likely to pay with money later.  
4. After consuming the magnet, they should **want more** of what you offer.

## Seven steps (from $100M Leads)

1. Figure out the **problem** and **who**  
2. Figure out **how to solve** it  
3. Figure out **how to deliver** it  
4. Test **what to name** it  
5. Make it **easy to consume**  
6. Make it **darn good**  
7. Make it easy to say they **want more** (CTA / next step)

## Problem–solution cycle

Every problem has a solution; every solution reveals more problems.  
Pick a **narrow + meaningful** problem → solve free → ensure the **next revealed problem** is what your core offer sells.

## Magnet types (conceptual)

- Reveal problems (audits, scores, assessments)  
- Partial solutions / samples  
- One complete sub-result (quick win)  

Rotate variations to keep advertising fresh.
